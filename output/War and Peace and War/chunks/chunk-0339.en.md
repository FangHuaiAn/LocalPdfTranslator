In addition to French, English, and southern Italian “Normandies,” there was also Iceland. Iceland was an isolated island with no external enemies. The Norse who settled there evolved a society that got along without the social discipline of either lordship or coercive kin groups. They managed a successful survival with the nuclear family, a weak sense of obligation to an extended kin, and with next to no government at all—all because they had no external enemies (see Searle, 1988:162).
