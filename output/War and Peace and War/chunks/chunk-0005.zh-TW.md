A science of history sounds cold and hard—wouldn’t it destroy our enjoyment of the wonderfully rich tapestry of the past? 在歷史學的觀點來看，顯得冰冷而刻板——這 wouldn't it 摧毀我們對過去那種豐富多彩的圖案的享受？

On a darker side, might not such a science enable some shadowy cabal to manipulate societies to a nefarious purpose? 而且，這種學科是否能使一些陰影中的勢力操縱社會以達到邪惡的目的？

But have we ceased to enjoy the blue sky of a brilliant summer day, or the play of colors in a glorious sunset? 但我們是否已經停止欣賞明媚夏日藍天，或在壯麗的日落中欣賞色彩的變化？

After all, the physicists, beginning with Newton and ending with Einstein, worked out exactly how colors of the sky result from the interaction of sunlight with the atmosphere. 畢竟，從牛頓到愛因斯坦開始並結束的物理學家們已經確切地闡述了天空顏色是如何通過陽光與大氣層相互作用而產生的。

As to the nefarious uses of a science of history, it is true that any knowledge can be turned to good or bad ends. 關於歷史學的邪惡用途，確實是說任何知識都可能被用於善或惡的目的。

But Asimov’s notion of a Second Foundation—a group of psychohistorians pulling the strings from some secret center—was always the least credible part of his vision. 但阿西莫夫對“第二基礎”——一群從某個秘密中心拉動琴弦的心理歷史學家們的想法，一直是他的願景中最不可信的部分。

War and Peace and War addresses the question raised by Asimov (and many other people before him, including Marx and Tolstoy): Is a science of history possible? 《戰爭與和平》探討了阿西莫夫（以及在他之前許多人，包括馬克思和托爾斯泰）提出的問題：歷史學是否可能？

Can we design a theory for the collapse of mighty empires that would be no worse than, say, our understanding of why earthquakes happen? 我們能否設計一種解釋強大帝國崩潰的理論，而且這個理論不會比我們對地震發生的原因理解差多少？

Seismologists have made great strides in understanding earthquakes. They can even make some limited predictions as to which areas of the earth are likely to be hit next by an earthquake. 地震學家在了解地震方面取得了很大進展。他們甚至可以對哪些地區最有可能受到地震襲擊做出一些有限的預測。

However, forecasting the precise timing and magnitude of an earthquake eludes them. 然而，準確預測地震的時間和規模仍然逃不過他們的魔爪。

Can a science of history, similarly, explain why states crumble, and perhaps predict which societies are in the danger of collapse? 歷史學是否也能夠像這樣，解釋國家為何崩潰，並可能預測哪些社會面臨崩潰的危險？

THIS BOOK FOCUSES ON EMPIRES. 這本書專注於帝國。

Why did some—initially small and insignificant—nations go on to build mighty empires, whereas other nations failed to do so? 一些——最初很小且不重要的——國家是如何發展成強大的帝國，而另一些國家卻未能做到這一點的？

And why do the successful empire builders invariably, given enough time, lose their empires? 並且，成功的帝國建設者不可避免地，在足夠的時間內失去他們的帝國嗎？

Can we understand how imperial powers rise and why they fall? 我們能否理解帝國勢力如何崛起以及它們為何衰落？

An empire is a large, multiethnic territorial state with a complex power structure. 帝國是一個擁有複雜權力結構的大型、多民族的領土國家。

The key variable is the size. 關鍵變數是規模。

When large enough, states invariably encompass ethnically diverse people; this makes them into multiethnic states. 當足夠大時，國家不可避免地包含來自不同族群的人；這使它們成為多民族國家。

And given the difficulties of communication in pre-industrial times, large states had to come up with a variety of ad hoc ways to bind far-flung territories to the center. 並且，在工業時代之前，由於溝通的困難，大型國家不得不想出各種臨時解決方案來將遙遠的地區與中心聯繫起來。

One of the typical expedients was to incorporate smaller neighbors as self-contained units, imposing tribute on them and taking over their foreign relations, but otherwise leaving their internal functioning alone. 其中一種常見的手段是將較小的鄰國納入自為運作的單位，對其徵稅並接管其外交關係，但除此之外，仍然讓它們的內部運作不受干擾。

Such a process of piecemeal accumulation usually leads to complicated chains of command and the coexistence of heterogeneous territories within one state. 這種零散積累的過程通常導致複雜的指揮系統和同一國家的內部分別存在多樣化領域共存。
