innovation. An early F-104A-10-LO (56-0763, once a chase aircraft
for the SR-71A, XB-70 Valkyrie and North American X-15 rocket-

with a modified J79 engine, custom-built suspension and solid aluminium
wheels, was being prepared for an attempt to break the 763-mph speed
record on land.
Meanwhile, at NASA’s Kennedy Space Center, Rick Svetkoff’s
Starfighters Aerospace operation uses his privately-owned fleet of nine
F-104s for a variety of research and development programmes in support
of the US government, academic institutions and commercial space
ventures. Like NASA’s now-retired F-104Ns, used for many years to train
astronauts, Rick’s F-104s are uniquely suited to this task. So much so that
Starfighters Aerospace has the only Federal Aviation Administration-
certified astronaut training programme in North America. In Svetkoff’s
words, ‘We can go from ground to 23,000 ft as fast as some of the rockets
launched here. High altitude and high speed is the key’.
These thoughts were also foremost in the mind of Clarence ‘Kelly’
Johnson some 61 years previously when he began to design the F-104
after USAF fighter pilots in Korea discovered that they needed much
better performance for consistent air superiority over communist
MiG-15s. The 1950s engendered unprecedentedly rapid development
and change, both in aviation technology and defence requirements. The
unexpected appearance of Soviet MiG-15 fighters in Korea in November
1950, when the Cold War turned hot, had kick-started a surge of fighter
development to restore American aerial superiority. Aircraft designers had
to devise complex solutions to unforeseen military situations in record
time, requiring enormous expenditure and designers with vision, daring
and talent approaching genius level.
Clarence ‘Kelly’ Johnson at the Lockheed Aircraft Company was one
of a number of American designers with all these qualities. He had created
the P-38 Lightning long-range interceptor, an innovative twin-boomed
aircraft with a distinguished World War 2 record. Experience with
compressibility early in the P-38’s life showed Johnson that totally new
concepts using jet power would be needed for significant increases in
speed. Merely doubling the Lightning’s power had increased top speed by
only 17 mph. Johnson began jet fighter studies in 1939, and by 1942 he
had a firm proposal, the L-133-02-01 with an advanced canard planform
and two L-1000 jet engines designed by Lockheed’s Nate Price. However,
the USAAF considered it too challenging a project for them to sponsor.
In 1942 the Bell Aircraft Corporation produced America’s first jet
fighter, the P-59 Airacomet, but its low-powered General Electric
J31-GE-5 engines in a high-drag airframe severely compromised its
performance. The unexpected appearance of the Messerschmitt Me 262
6 jet fighter over Germany changed USAAF perceptions overnight, and a

The designers trialled various configurations before settling on a high
‘T-tail’ empennage arrangement. Extensive wind tunnel testing and free-
flight investigation using 5-in rockets led to the conclusion that a T-tail
minimised drag and provided the best stability and control across the
broad speed and altitude envelope of the F-104. The placement of the
horizontal stabiliser decreased interference drag and minimised trim
change in the transonic and supersonic regimes. While most aircraft
become nose-heavy as they transition to supersonic speed, the F-104
would experience only negligible trim changes, markedly improving
manoeuvrability. The horizontal stabiliser acted as an end plate for the
vertical tail, increasing the effectiveness of the relatively small tail while
providing a positive dihedral effect. In fact the tail’s dihedral effect proved
too powerful, and after testing with Calspan’s NT-33A variable stability
aircraft, a ten-degree anhedral was applied to the Starfighter’s wings to
improve handling characteristics.

At subsonic speeds the wing itself provided enough stall warning even
for novice pilots. However, at supersonic speeds there was no warning of
an impending stall via the usual aerodynamic signals of heavy buffet and
a tendency to roll, so a warning system was indeed necessary. The F-104
Pilot’s Manual prohibited pitch-up, not because it was inherently
dangerous (the F-104 refused to spin in all but the most extreme
conditions), but because the aircraft’s gyrations after a stall could damage
the airframe. When it pitched down the aircraft could swing violently
sideways into the airflow and the tail would then flip it from a right to a
left roll. This sudden reversal caused several cases of twisted fuselages
during F-104 flight tests to explore spin characteristics.
Early pilots emphasised that despite its unusual configuration the
Starfighter essentially behaved like a conventional aircraft, but it required
careful, smooth handling and would not tolerate inattentive or ‘ham-
fisted’ piloting. In service use the findings of these early tests continued
to provide an effective guide to safe flying for F-104 pilots, but failure to
observe them undoubtedly became a major cause of the aircraft’s
unenviable safety record with several foreign air forces. As ‘Jake’ Holliman
commented, ‘Treat her rough and you may have a miserable time. Be
smooth and gentle and you’re in for a very pleasant experience. “Pitch-up”
doesn’t mean “give-up” if you have sufficient altitude to wait it out’.
MACH 2 POWER
Basic to all the F-104’s qualities was the General Electric J79 variable-
stator, axial-flow engine. When the first XF-104 (53-7786) made its
maiden flight, it and the second prototype had to use a Buick-manufactured
Wright J65-B-3 – a licence-built British Armstrong Siddeley Sapphire.
This engine had an afterburner installed later in the programme as the
J65-W-6, but the first XF-104 had already gone supersonic in a dive with
the non-afterburning J65-B-3. Development of the revolutionary X-24A
or J79 (the first engine capable of Mach 2 flight) ran contemporaneously
with that of the F-104 as General Electric engineers led by Neil Burgess
and Gerhard Neumann worked on the problems of achieving rapid
acceleration without compressor stalling, low fuel consumption and
reliable performance throughout a wide speed range.
The engine commenced test runs in June 1954 and a J79-GE-3 was
installed in the first of 15 YF-104As (59-2955), which undertook its
maiden flight on 17 February 1956. YF-104As had substantial structural
changes, including a 5 ft 9 in fuselage extension from the XF-104’s 49 ft
2 in to accommodate more fuel, a forward-retracting nose undercarriage
and a revised rear fuselage with a ventral fin to aid stability.
Major revisions to the air inlets to cope with the extra power of the J79
introduced half-cone centre-bodies to generate multiple shockwaves at
supersonic speeds. Incoming air had to be slowed to below Mach 1 to
avoid choking the engine, and some of it by-passed the J79 and was used
to cool the afterburner. The intakes were optimised for Mach 1.7 (or
Mach 2 in versions with the more powerful J79-GE-19 engine) and the
10 aircraft generated its maximum excess thrust at that speed. The J79

High landing speed was another corollary of the small wing area, and
this was ameliorated to some extent by the use of Attinello-type blown
flaps from the YF-104A onwards. Bleed air from the engine’s 17th
compressor stage was blown across the flaps to provide boundary layer
control (BLC) at increasing pressure through 55 slots in titanium ducts
when flaps were lowered between 15 and 45 degrees, reducing landing
speed by more than 30 knots. Pilots also had to be ready to pull the flaps
back to 15 degrees if there was a malfunction in the flap-blowing to one
wing, causing an immediate roll at a dangerous moment in the landing
approach. Supplying sufficient airflow to the flap-blowing system required
high engine power while landing. A dead-stick landing without flap-
blowing was possible, but with a touch-down speed of 240 knots very
accurate judgement was crucial.
All F-104 versions had two flap settings. For take off, both leading and
trailing edge flaps dropped to 15 degrees without BLC. For landing, the
leading edge flaps dropped to 30 degrees and the trailing edge flaps to 45
degrees, with BLC applied progressively as the flaps extended past 15
degrees in the ‘land’ position. Pilots could also select the ‘take off’ (15
degrees) setting as ‘manoeuvre flaps’ at higher speeds to improve turn rate
by about 1g. Pilots often used this facility, but with discretion to prevent
undue loss of speed in combat with better-turning fighters.
The choice of a high-T tailplane out of 290 possible airframe
configurations also raised the concern that no existing cartridge-driven
ejection seat would be powerful enough to avoid impacting the tail at high
speed up to Mach 2 without using a cartridge that would injure a pilot’s
back due to its extreme acceleration. Having rejected the idea of a self-
contained escape capsule, the designers, therefore, and with some
reservations, included a seat that fired downwards through a hatch (which
formed the cockpit floor) once the cockpit had been de-pressurised, the
control column retracted and the pilot’s legs pulled back against the seat.
The first seats for the prototypes and the first 26 F-104As were Stanley
B downward-ejection models with an M3 catapult initiator. These were
replaced by the Stanley C and later C-1 with cables attached to ‘spurs’ on
the pilot’s boots to pull his feet back safely against the seat front, while
knee guards rotated downwards for further protection. 11

Pilots of the F-104 and its contemporaries relied on ground control

visual meteorological conditions-only fighter’.

F-104 pilot guided into a supersonic stern attack on an intruder at about

COMBAT CAPABILITY
The combination of the short, straight wing and T-tail made the Starfighter
a very responsive aircraft, albeit one that would only ‘wake up’ at speeds
above 300-350 knots. A good F-104 pilot would not ‘get slow’, but would
instead exploit the aircraft’s high thrust/weight ratio to accelerate quickly
into a fight (often unobserved due to the F-104’s small visual signature)
and use the excess thrust in the vertical dimension. Because the jet could
out-accelerate every contemporary enemy aircraft and could readily use
its top speed advantage, especially at medium and low altitudes, a pilot
could choose to engage and disengage combat at will.
Also, the F-104’s low-drag, efficient engine/airframe match and
generous internal fuel capacity gave it a good combat endurance so it
could normally outlast an opponent. An F-104 could fly over enemy
territory using the fuel in its drop tanks, jettison those tanks for aerial
combat, engage in low-altitude combat for five minutes at maximum
thrust, fly home and have sufficient reserves for a safe landing. Notably,
the Starfighter’s low drag gave it a high combat cruise speed at intermediate
throttle settings.
Indeed, an F-104A with two wingtip AIM-9Bs could easily reach Mach
2, whereas with two tip-tanks speed was limited to around Mach 1.7. In
either case, the fighter could provide very effective air superiority cover.
After the F-104’s service entry air-to-air tactics were developed, ranging
from simple single-ship, ‘slash-and-run’ attacks or high ‘yo-yos’ against
less-capable adversaries, to more sophisticated coordinated double attack
tactics, which at the time were used exclusively by the Starfighter
community. Pilots could tailor their tactics to their opponents. Against a
lower-performance aircraft like the MiG-17, the F-104 pilot could afford
to let speed bleed down to 350 knots in manoeuvres, while a MiG-19
would demand at least 450 knots and a MiG-21 would dictate a 600-knot
minimum. Because of the proximity of George AFB, in California, to
Nellis AFB, Nevada, George’s F-104Cs (assigned to TAC’s 479th Tactical
Fighter Wing) often supported the USAF’s Fighter Weapon School (FWS)

F-104s WITH ‘NUKES’
The F-104C version was originally intended to replace some of TAC’s
F-100 Super Sabres, offering much higher speed in Low Altitude Bombing
System (LABS) ‘toss bomb’ attacks with nuclear weapons (increasing its
chances of safe escape) and superior air-to-air qualities. Equipping fighters
to enhance Strategic Air Command’s dominant nuclear influence was also
a way of sustaining TAC’s budget at that time.
Development of TAC’s Starfighter, substantially funded by Lockheed
in the hope of larger orders, took 18 months. It had the up-rated
J79-GE-7A engine, with a two-inch increase in turbine diameter,
generating 15,800 lbs of afterburning thrust. Blown flaps were installed
from the outset and range was increased by plumbing in an external,
detachable refuelling probe which had been tested on F-104As ahead of
the Operation Jonah Able deployment to Taiwan in September 1958. Its
nozzle was placed conveniently within the pilot’s left-side vision, making
refuelling from the temperamental trailing basket of a KC-97 tanker a
relatively simple five-minute operation. As former F-104C pilot Norm
Filer explained;
‘The boom was just a pipe with the nozzle at one end and a fitting to
replace the fuel cap at the other. The refuelling light was in a small fairing
at the base of the boom and the fairing that covered the pipe from the
light aft was a simple, somewhat flimsy, fibreglass part with a million
screws into the fuselage.’
For its tactical roles the F-104C was wired for a 2000-lb ordnance
load such as a Mk 28 or Mk 43 nuclear store on a short centreline pylon.
Two 195-gallon tanks or ordnance, typically two 750-lb M117s or
(for the F-104G) 1000-lb bombs, could be hung on the two underwing
pylons, as could other TAC stores including BLU-1 fire-bombs or
LAU-3 rocket pods. The M61 cannon was re-installed, and from
October 1961 the ordnance upgrade programme Project Grindstone
added a twin-Sidewinder ‘catamaran’ launcher beneath the fuselage, thus
allowing the carriage of wingtip fuel tanks. This facility was not used
operationally by USAF F-104Cs as the launcher increased drag and
could not be jettisoned.
An upgraded AN/ASG-14T-2 fire control system (FCS) gave improved
night target acquisition, but like other TAC fighters of the time, the
F-104C was limited by its visual-only weapons. However, the FCS
‘switchology’ remained very simple to operate, particularly in comparison
with the systems in the F-105D Thunderchief and F-4C Phantom II,
leaving the pilot free to concentrate on both flying the jet and on keeping
an adversary in sight. An overall structural beef-up to cope with the extra
weight (23,590 lbs maximum) required 36 new metal forgings for
18 the airframe.

There were extended delays in the F-104C delivery process caused
mainly by development problems with the J79 engine and M61 gun
installation. Engine compressor stalls, afterburner failure due to the jet
nozzle ‘petals’ sticking in the open position and reducing thrust,
together with engine overheating, were all encountered during the
YF-104 test programme. A grounding order for all F-104As was
enforced in April 1958 while improved J79-GE-3B engines were
installed, further postponing the type’s service introduction. TAC’s
interest in the F-104 had by then begun to decline at a time of rapidly
changing defence policies. From 1956 it sought larger, longer-ranged
fighters with bigger ordnance loads. Further emphasis on TAC’s
conventional warfare capability at the end of the 1950s reinforced its
need for fighters that could carry nuclear weapons or a more substantial
conventional weapons load, rather than the F-104C with its limited
ground attack role.
The Starfighter order was, therefore, limited to 77 aircraft – enough for
only one TAC wing. If the aircraft had become embroiled in a European
nuclear war scenario, on which much of TAC’s planning centred, it is
likely that the F-104Cs would have been used in the air superiority role
while the nuclear missions would have been allocated to F-100Ds or
F-105Ds.
The first F-104C flew on 24 July 1958 and deliveries to the
476th TFS/479th TFW at George AFB began in September of that year,
replacing the unit’s F-100 Super Sabres. Although its use was limited to
the four George-based squadrons, the Starfighter had a far more useful,
active and varied combat career than TAC planners might have guessed
in 1958. Twenty-one F-104D trainers were also delivered, with a
two-cockpit nose added to the F-104C airframe without the M61 gun or
centreline stores provision. A revised canopy had a larger fixed
centre-section and windscreen between the two opening transparencies,
later retro-fitted to some early F-104Bs to coincide with the installation
of upward-firing ejection seats.
F-104Ds also had in-flight refuelling probes and 15,800-lb thrust
J79-GE-7 engines. The latter was the subject of a programme by General
Electric, TAC and the Oklahoma City Air Materiel Center in 1960-61 to
improve the reliability of the F-104/J79 combination using re-designed
compressor blades.
Each F-104C squadron usually had two F-104Ds on strength for
transition and proficiency training, and they had the speed and climb
performance to match the single-seaters.

GUARDING EUROPE – THE BERLIN CRISIS
Infuriated by the outflow of more than two million East Germans to the
West in search of better lives, and by the refusal of America, Britain and
France to accept an end to the four-power treaty that allowed them access
to West Berlin, Soviet Premier Nikita Khrushchev threatened to use force
to deny any possibility of a re-united Germany. On 13 August 1961 he
sealed off most of the border-crossing points between East and West
Berlin and constructed a wall to divide the city. America’s military strategy
was focused on a nuclear response, but in this case it was clear that a show
of conventional force was required.
The lack of frontline conventional tactical air power meant that ANG
squadrons were required to deploy to Germany in a situation in which
President John F Kennedy wanted to ‘have a wider choice than humiliation
or all-out nuclear action’. Eleven ANG fighter squadrons were sent to
Germany, France and Spain to support nuclear-capable USAFE tactical
units, preceded by elements of the 479th TFW with nuclear-capable
F-104Cs. Another 11 ANG squadrons were mobilised on short-notice
alert to deploy as part of Operation Stair Step (the codename for the rapid
aerial movement of the fighters to Europe). Among them were the three
F-104A units, mobilised on 1 October.
Once again the MATS C-124 transport force was called in to airlift 60
F-104As to West Germany in November in Operation Brass Ring, as it
seemed likely that Khrushchev would blockade West Berlin. The 197th
FIS took 18 F-104As and two F-104Bs to Ramstein AB, West Germany,
where it was joined by a similar number from the 151st FIS of the
Tennessee ANG. They were both assigned to the 86th Air (Defence)
Division of the Seventeenth Air Force and the Tennessee crews assumed
alert duty from 19 December. The Arizona pilots flew patrols towards and
along the border with the Soviet bloc until August 1962. Most pilots had
to visit Wheelus AB, in Libya, to qualify on live-firing the AIM-9B missile
before they were able to perform alert duties in Germany. Capt Gene

GROUND ATTACK MISSIONS
The F-104C’s ground attack targets were in South Vietnam or on the
Laotian supply trails network, pilots seeking out and bombing truck parks
or suspected supply dumps and troop concentrations. Korean War veteran
Maj Norman Schmidt, flying F-104C 57-0913 (‘Fresno 1’) on 1
September 1966, was flying an armed reconnaissance sortie and bombing
a truck park near Troc when AAA struck his aircraft as he climbed away.
The F-104C survived for only ten miles and Schmidt had to eject over
coastal flatlands south of Dong Hoi. He was captured and sent to the
notorious ‘Desert Inn’ section of Hoa Lo prison, where he was apparently
beaten to death by a brutal guard in August 1967.
On a more positive note, on 30 September 1966 Capt Harold Alston
of the 435th TFS became the first Starfighter pilot to fly 100 missions
over North Vietnam. That same day squadronmate Capt Mike Korte
(nicknamed ‘The Mellow Indian’) flew the 1000th F-104 sortie over the
North. Capt Alston was presented with a commemorative plaque by
Lockheed test pilot Tony Le Vier, the first person to fly the F-104. A State
Department recommendation that the unit should receive an award for
this achievement was not acted upon by Seventh Air Force commander
Gen William Momyer, however, and the statistics were then logged
incorrectly in official records as figures for the entire tour.
Two more losses in October underlined the hazardous nature of missions
in the Barrel Roll campaign over Laos, and also signalled the end of the
F-104Cs’ participation in them as 1966 drew to a close. Pilots had gained
a reputation for considerable accuracy with their limited ordnance loads at
a time when the USAF tended to assess fighters by their ordnance-carrying
capacity. Although less concentrated than in the Hanoi-Haiphong complex,

In 1948, after the partition of India and Pakistan and the initial clash
over Kashmir between the two countries, the Indian Air Force (IAF)
became the first Asian air arm with jet fighters (de Havilland Vampires),

aircraft. Pilots for the elite No 9 Sqn ‘Griffins’ came with considerable
flying time on the F-86F Sabre. Groundcrews, with long experience on

him an excellent pilot, although he could never beat Capt Hood at ping-

During the third and final week of the war the Sargodha Starfighters
maintained their very effective defensive posture. Rather like the USAF’s

approaching F-104A.

THE THIRTEEN-DAY WAR
Lacking intervention from the international community, India (which had
signed a defence pact with the Soviet Union in August 1971) decided in
December 1971 to intervene in the conflict over Bangladesh and began a
massive build-up of forces along the East Pakistan border. Air action had
actually begun on 16 October when IAF Canberras attacked targets in West
Pakistan. Around 40 Canberras remained in Indian service, and they had
been joined by around 175 MiG-21FL ‘Trishuls’ (Tridents) equipping nine
squadrons. There were also 150 Sukhoi Su-7BMs, 160 Gnats, 100 Hunters,
domestically produced HF-24 Maruts and 40 Mystères, allowing the IAF
to field around 730 combat aircraft. Of these, the Hunters and Su-7BMs
would sustain the majority of the casualties during the renewed conflict.
Pakistan, deprived of Western re-armament by embargoes, had acquired
70 Sidewinder-armed Chinese Shenyang F-6s (MiG-19s) and 90
ex-Luftwaffe Canadair Sabre 6s via Iran. The Chinese deal was allegedly
connected to the loan (and eventual return) of a disassembled PAF F-104A
to China for technical examination. France also supplied 18 Mirage IIIEPs
to replace F-104As, and in September 1970 the USA offered a ‘one-time
exception’ arms deal that included seven more F-104As, but at that
stage the PAF was already looking to phase out its Starfighters. Indeed,
the pilots of No 9 Sqn had been preparing for new aircraft, as Farooq

HASHEMITE STARS
The ‘Griffins’’ move to Masroor air base near Karachi was followed by the
loan of Jordanian aircraft. King Hussein had apparently received urgent
Pakistani requests for this aid, and he sought American approval, which
could not be given legally. On 12 December 1971 Gen Alexander M
Haig, Deputy Assistant to the President for National Security Affairs, told
Ambassador Huang Hua, the Permanent Representative to the UN of the
Peoples’ Republic of China, that Jordan ‘has sent six fighter aircraft to
Pakistan, and intends to send others up to a total of 14’. The latter would
be replaced within the RJAF by fighters sent from Iran. On 19 January
1972 US National Security Advisor Henry Kissinger told President
Richard Nixon that Jordan had sent 17 aircraft in all.
Kissinger regarded India as a ‘Russian satellite’ at the time, and he
therefore sought covert ways of assisting Pakistan. The US Ambassador
in Pakistan had also warned that the ‘trickle’ of Chinese MiG-19s and
Jordanian F-104s arriving in Pakistan would be inadequate if India made
an all-out attack on its neighbour.
Five RJAF F-104As were flown in by Jordanian and PAF pilots on 13
December and another five followed a short while later. Maj Ihsan
Shurdom (later the commander-in-chief of the RJAF) led the first RJAF
Starfighters into Masroor on 13 December, and the aircraft were to remain
in Pakistan with PAF markings until three weeks after the war. Political
constraints officially prevented them from crossing the Indian border on
combat missions, however.
On 26 December three camouflaged Northrop F-5As in PAF markings
passed through Tehran airport en route to Pakistan. They were part of a
batch of eight originally destined for Libya but diverted from storage in
California to Pakistan, via Iran and Turkey. It is conceivable that they were
used in combat since Indian troops claimed to have discovered camouflaged
wreckage that they stated was from an F-104. PAF and RJAF Starfighters
were not camouflaged, unlike F-5As.
Pakistan’s 1971 air war was essentially defensive, as the PAF’s inability
to secure air superiority over India due to a lack of fighters severely limited
the number of air attacks it could mount on enemy forces. Airfields were
hit whenever possible to try and reduce the IAF’s considerable numerical
advantage, and F-104As were used for their speed advantage, although
their ordnance limitations confined them to strafing attacks, with
AIM-9Bs for self-defence.
One such mission was against the heavily defended Jamnagar air base on
13 December. Thirty-one-year-old Wg Cdr Mervyn L Middlecoat (in
F-104A 56-0773) led Flt Lt Tariq Habib at low level towards the target
before climbing to 3000 ft to begin a strafing dive. Middlecoat, who had
already made three reconnaissance flights over Indian airfields in recent days
and completed 17 air defence sorties in 1965, had become the CO of No
9 Sqn during 1971. Positioning for the strafing run took several minutes,
giving the defences additional preparation time. A missile was fired at
