Arriving on station, the aircraft divided into pairs, orbiting at 8000 ft

them, turning away at four miles range when the CAP aircraft turned to

Capt Gene West participated in Bolo;
‘Bolo wasn’t a big surprise. I often wondered why it took so long! It was
scheduled for 1 January but the weather was really bad, so it was
re-scheduled for the next day. We were first advised of the mission, which
was to provide top cover for the F-4Cs, that morning. Only high-time
F-104 pilots were assigned to this operation, and I was one of the 12 to
go. Bob Preciado led and I flew as No 3 in Col Ed Gaines’ flight. He was
at Seventh Air Force HQ and often came down to fly missions with us
– a real fighter pilot’s pilot!
‘We got airborne and headed north. When we crossed into Laos,
Preciado had a navigation problem so Gaines took the lead. The weather
was not the greatest, with visibility of one to two miles and cloud up to
8000 ft. The red SAM-warning “launch” light on my RHAW equipment
stayed on during the entire mission. A number of MiGs “bought the farm”
and I would say we had a very good day up North.’
Capt Gene West’s 28 January 1967 Silver Dawn/Big Eye flight with Lt
Travis Harrell proved to be less successful, however. Indeed, West endured
a sequence of mishaps that he was lucky to survive. While refuelling he
saw a Master Caution ‘low oil’ indication and the oil pressure rapidly
dropped to zero. West’s unsuccessful attempts to close the jet nozzle with
the emergency ‘T’ handle in order to gain some power were followed by
a brief afterburner re-light and a complete failure of the engine oil system,
causing it to rumble, grind and soon seize up. As Gene ruefully
commented, ‘when the engine quits, there is silence’. He was obliged to
eject as the F-104 descended to 8000 ft around 25 miles offshore.
The survival kit attached to West’s seat was damaged so that his liferaft
was not inflated when he hit the water. Disentangling himself from his
parachute meant swallowing a lot of Tonkin water. A ship passed quite close
to him, but he could not contact it on his RT-10 radio, and then the radio
was lost in a heavy wave while he tried to get hold of a flare – which did not
ignite. After 20 minutes’ effort West managed to retrieve his second radio,
deploy his seat survival kit and inflate the raft by placing both feet on the
kit and pulling mightily on the release handle. His second radio
malfunctioned, but he was able to indicate his position to an A-1E RESCAP
aircraft by keying the microphone. After several close passes a rescue
helicopter located him visually, although the cloud ceiling was at 50 ft and
visibility was restricted to 200 ft. He returned to Udorn uninjured.
