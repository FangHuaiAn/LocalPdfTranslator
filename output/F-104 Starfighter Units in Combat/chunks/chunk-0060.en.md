However, some sorties over the North still took place in the spring of
1967, with Gene logging a night mission near Haiphong. On 10 March
– the day that the Thai Nguyen steel mill was cleared for attack, and also
the day of ‘Pardo’s Push’, in which one F-4 literally pushed another
powerless F-4 to a safe bail-out area – an F-104C came close to having an
aerial collision with 469th TFS strike flight F-105D 60-0423. The pilot
of the latter machine, Capt Clint Murphy, recalled;
‘I came as close today as I ever will to having a mid-air. We were
manoeuvring around a tanker. As I looked back, in front of me an F-104
passed head-on not more than 20 ft below me. With about 1200 mph
closure rate I didn’t even have time to react.’
Withdrawal of the F-104Cs after 5306 combat sorties, and their
replacement by the F-4D Phantom IIs of the 4th TFS ‘Fightin’ Fuujins’
from Eglin AFB’s 33rd TFW, was justified by the general phasing out of
USAF Starfighters (and a consequent shortage of parts, particularly M61
spares, and support) and the need to preserve the remaining F-104Cs in
case they were required to meet a MiG threat in future. The aircraft’s small
ordnance load compared with the F-4 was also cited.
Kelly Johnson’s verdict on its wartime service was that ‘the F-104 went
to Vietnam but did not get into [air-to-air] combat. It was not directed to
get into combat. It was directed to stay high and provide protection. The
people who flew it over there thought very highly of it, but there were many
pressures to get a new aeroplane going in the US – the F-15 in particular.’
With the ending of F-104 operations at George AFB in July 1967, the
returning 435th TFS aircraft needed a new home. They flew to San Juan,
in Puerto Rico, via the Philippines, Guam and Hawaii for service with the
198th TFS ‘Flying Buccaneers’ – the only ANG unit to fly the F-104C.
For the remainder of the Vietnam War, particularly the Linebacker phase
in 1971-72, it was planned that many of the 70 surviving F-104C/Ds could
be quickly recalled to combat if required, as enough would have been kept
at operational readiness by Puerto Rico’s ‘Bucaneros’. Four ‘PRANG’ pilots
were trained by the 436th TFS at George AFB in April 1967 and F-104C/D
Starfighters were sent to Muñiz, in Puerto Rico, in August for continuation
training of 23 pilots. Refuelling support for the unit came from the KC-97Ls
of the 181st Air Refueling Squadron at NAS Dallas, Texas, and KC-135As
from McCoy AFB, Florida. They were often loaded with an extra pair of
