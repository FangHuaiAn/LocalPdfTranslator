India’s MiG-21s had been ordered in 1962 specifically to counter the
PAF’s F-104As, and they became the only option after France opposed
Indian licence-production of Mirage IIIs and the USA refused to supply
Starfighters to the IAF without political constraints on their use.
The USSR agreed to deliver ten MiG-21F-13s in 1963-64 (and trained
a core of seven pilots), followed by two MiG-21PFs in March 1965, all
for No 28 Sqn. It gave the IAF a fighter with similar performance and
ground-attack capability to the F-104A, although the squadron was still
completing training and had just eight serviceable aircraft at the outbreak
of war, so its role in that conflict was only small. In training, MiG-21
pilots of No 28 Sqn concentrated on the high-altitude interception for
which the aircraft was conceived, rather than the low and medium altitude
dogfighting that they would later encounter.
PAF Starfighters initially saw action during the fighting over the Rann
of Kutch, a mineral-rich area of India bordering Pakistan which Pakistani
forces entered in March 1965. Hostilities began in April, and two F-104As
were detached to Mauripur air base, from where they flew CAPs near the
Indian border. An IAF Canberra PR 57 on a reconnaissance mission was
intercepted and followed for ten minutes, but the Starfighter pilot was
told not to shoot at it.
