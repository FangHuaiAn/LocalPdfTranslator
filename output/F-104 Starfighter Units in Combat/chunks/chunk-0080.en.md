Lt Gen Tom Baker, a former instructor pilot at Luke AFB, emphasised
that the F-104 was essentially ‘easy to fly. It had some “squirrely”
characteristics, but it was okay if you were aware of them and stayed out
of those situations. There is no doubt that the inexperience of some senior
Luftwaffe officers in the early 1960s caused them to make decisions
regarding the operation of this very high-performance aeroplane in
Europe, with its associated poor weather conditions, that pushed a lot of
guys into situations that were high risk. A high accident rate was the end
product of these decisions.
‘Gen Johannes Steinhoff [Luftwaffe Chief of Staff] came to Luke AFB
searching for an answer. The accident rate here was not high, and it was
obvious that conditions in Western Europe were very different which
meant that pilots were not being prepared for frontline flying. One of
their “fixes” was to make pilots [in Europe] fly the maximum number of
hours per sortie, but numbers of hours was not the real answer. It was a
safe aeroplane if flown in the correct environment by experienced people.
If you degraded one of those factors then you were probably set up for a
high accident rate.’
Apart from sporadic combat encounters within NATO, the F-104’s role
was principally as a deterrent, as it had been in Vietnam, Taiwan and
Pakistan. In Air Vice Marshal Farooq Umar’s view, ‘my 1000 hours on the
Starfighter proved to my colleagues and I that the jet had an awesome
effect on its opponents. The F-104 could climb like a rocket and accelerate
like a bullet. Anytime the opponents saw or heard of a Starfighter in the
92 vicinity they hastily retreated, and didn’t engage the F-104.’
