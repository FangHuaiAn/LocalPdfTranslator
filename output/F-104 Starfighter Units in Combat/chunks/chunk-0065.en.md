Generally, ‘Griffin’ pilots noticed that the IAF would allow Starfighters
to overfly its bases without attempting interception. There were many
reports circulating at Sargodha of Hunter and Mystère pilots avoiding
engagement with the No 9 Sqn fighters and denying them the chance of
aerial combat. F-104As had their best fighting opportunities at lower
altitudes. Higher up, the larger wing area of IAF Hunters and Gnats gave
them superior manoeuvrability.
When the conflict spread to a full-scale war on 6 September the PAF
was accused of bombing the Sikh spiritual centre at Amritsar, but the
damage was later attributed to a reconnaissance F-104’s sonic boom. The
‘Griffins’ reverted to air defence and a dawn CAP that day, and Flt Lts
Aftab Alam and Amjad Hussain, patrolling at 15,000 ft, detected four
Mystère IVAs from No 1 ‘Tiger’ Sqn, led by Wg Cdr ‘Omi’ Taneja,
attacking railway fuel tankers near Ghakkhar. Mystère pilot Sqn Ldr
‘Paddy’ Earle saw the F-104As diving on them and his formation headed
for the border on the deck at 450 knots, with Aftab Alam (who had
jettisoned his wing tanks) closing rapidly behind them, guided by the
Sakesar radar air defence controller. His wingman remained at altitude as
radio relay. Aftab Alam later reported;
‘Manoeuvring started at treetop level. I kept my eyes glued on the target.
I could feel the strain under high-gs, looking over the tail of the aircraft as
I skimmed the trees at high speed. One mistake and I would have hit the
ground. The F-104A, with afterburner blazing, was responding very well.
I used the flaps to improve the turning capability as required. The “stick
shaker” was a big help in flying the aircraft to its limits. I armed my weapons
and decided to shoot the first two [Mystères] with missiles and the next two
with guns. I fully realised that a confidential order prohibited me from using
the missiles below 10,000 ft. However, I was confident that they could be
used at any height provided the target could be discriminated from
background heat sources. A distinct increase in missile tone ensured this.’
Flt Lt Aftab Alam set up a Sidewinder, firing at the nearest Mystère and
then zoom-climbing, dazzled by the flash of the missile launch so that he
did not see an impact. Although Pakistani forces detected a fire and
explosion on the ground near Rahwali airfield, and radio transmissions
from the Mystères reported a shoot-down, the IAF stated that the fire
resulted from burning fuel in drop tanks jettisoned from Earle’s Mystère.
However, the PAF claimed this as its first kill by an F-104A, as well as
Pakistan’s first missile kill.
Attacks on PAF air bases began on 7 September when Flt Lt Amjad
Hussain’s F-104A was in a CAP over Sargodha, which turned to meet six
‘Tigers’’ Mystères that were about to attack the ‘Griffins’’ home base. He
pursued the formation, caught them about eight miles from Sargodha and
fired an AIM-9B at an altitude of about 100 ft. The missile dived into the
ground and he closed to within gun range, seeing 20 mm hits on a Mystère.
A second Mystère turned towards Amjad Hussain, forcing him to climb to
around 15,000 ft in a high-speed yo-yo manoeuvre so that he could
re-attack. Closing once again to gun range (3000 ft), he fired his Vulcan
cannon at the IAF fighter, which exploded about 1000 ft ahead of him.
