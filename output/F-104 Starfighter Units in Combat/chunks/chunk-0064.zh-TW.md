Later, F-104As provided top-cover for the PAF’s few No 20 Sqn RT-33A
photo-reconnaissance flights, safeguarding them from interception at
their typical speeds of less than 400 knots, but having to cope with the
considerable speed differential by constant manoeuvring.
The two PAF F-104Bs also flew reconnaissance missions, roaring along
at very low altitude close to 600 knots, often in advance of bombing
missions by B-57Bs. Photographs were taken with a hand-held camera by
the back-seat crew member, sometimes with the aircraft inverted.
Although the results were not equal to those obtained with the type of
reconnaissance pod used with the F-104G, the Starfighter’s inherent
stability at low altitude produced some useful images nevertheless. Farooq
Umar was one of the pilots who flew these hazardous missions;
‘Hand-held cameras were resorted to, and Flt Lts Middlecoat, Aftab
Alam and I did the photography in the two-seat jet. A camera with image
motion compensation was also tried out, and I was one of the pilots who
tested it. It worked very well, with clear resolution at high speed and at
altitudes as low as 150 ft above the ground.’
One of the F-104Bs was later modified with three TA7M cameras (one
vertical and two oblique) in the rear cockpit, although this installation
was not used operationally as three Mirage IIIRPs had been acquired by
then. These missions led to proposals for low-altitude strafing attacks on
IAF airfields up to 350 miles from Sargodha by F-104As equipped with
four external fuel tanks to provide adequate range. However, most of the
IAF aircraft destroyed by the PAF were bombed during night attacks on
