degrading effects of ground return limited
the use of Search Mode up to an altitude of
about 3000 ft. Closing to within ten miles
of the target, the radar could be switched
to Track Mode, using a track button on the
control column. The radar scan then
narrowed to 20 degrees and a range strobe
began to sweep between 300 and 3000
yards in auto-acquisition mode. Holding
down the track button set the range strobe
at the point where the button was released.
Pressing the button again made the sweep
range move back to zero. A pilot could
thereby set the range to coincide with the
target, and at that point a lock-on light
illuminated and the radar started to send
range information to the optical sight in
order to establish correct lead angles. On

The AN/ASG-14 radar was sufficiently powerful for the air superiority
mission, performed within visual range for which it was designed. The
concept of combat beyond visual range using much larger search radars
was still some years away.
Pilots of the F-104 and its contemporaries relied on ground control
radars for information on more distant targets. Lt Col David Andrews
recalled that in the F-104A as an interceptor ‘we flew routinely with
wingtip fuel tanks. Other external tanks were used for ferry or cross-
country purposes, with a consequent reduction in maximum speed. We
had a very small “window” in which to locate, attack and return to base.
I suspect the “powers that be” considered an F-104A intercept as a one-
time shot. We operated with and depended on ground-controlled
interception [GCI] for initial target acquisition, and we practised both
high altitude and low-level interceptions and we were not considered a
visual meteorological conditions-only fighter’.
Quick-reaction interceptions were set up by a GCI controller, with the
F-104 pilot guided into a supersonic stern attack on an intruder at about
120-150 miles from base. He would then start his return home with
around 1500 lbs of fuel still remaining.
