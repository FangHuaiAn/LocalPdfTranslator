VIETNAM ‘Leading our flight was Maj Charlie Wood, 435th TFS Assistant
Operations Officer. No 2 [in 56-0928] was Capt Jack Kwortnik on about
his fifth mission. He was a former C-140 JetStar pilot at Clark AFB and,
ironically, I had crewed with him on his first flight in an F-104D.
Understandably, he was a little apprehensive, and came to my room the
night before the mission to talk tactics, specifically about SA-2s. He knew
this was my second tour, and that I had come to Udorn with the advanced
party, flying up North for two months before Jack and the rest of the
squadron arrived in-theatre. I told him everything I had experienced,
missiles that I had seen and what worked to evade them. Additionally, I
stressed that he should write to his family often, not that fighter pilots
expect to be shot down, but the possibility of being hit by a “golden BB”
[AAA round] was present on every mission.
‘Capt “Howie” Sargent was No 3 in the flight, with me on his wing – we
felt this would put critical experience where we could keep an eye on the
least experienced pilot. We flew in combat formation at between 4000-8000 ft
for a little terrain masking from enemy radar, but mostly to have the Weasels
in position and ready to fire their anti-radar missiles or strafe SAM sites. The
only problem was that this exposed us to almost every gun and SA-2 in the
area. Also, there was not a lot of manoeuvring space below us.
‘We entered our patrol area a little north of “Thud Ridge”, northwest
of Hanoi. Almost immediately the Weasels received enemy radar activity.
We were in an easy right turn eastwards when they got a SAM launch
signal. They called it out to us, saying “Take it down!” The first SAM
came from our right rear “four o’clock” position. We were in a left-hand
fingertip formation – each aircraft about 1500 ft from an element leader,
with me as No 4 on the left – about 4000 ft above a horseshoe-shaped
valley surrounded by mountains, opening towards Hanoi. The Weasels
were about 6000 ft ahead of us.
‘I had a great view of the “Thuds”, our flight and the area to the right
and behind – my area of responsibility to clear visually. That is exactly
where the SAM came from. I didn’t see it launched but picked it up
visually coming our way. It appeared to be tracking on Jack on the right
of the formation, not on the Weasels. I did not feel threatened, but did
not know how many more had been fired.
‘When the Weasels had said “take it down” we pushed the nose down
in a negative G “bunt” for the fastest change in altitude to take us nearer
the ground and make it more difficult for the enemy radar to track us.
Jack rolled inverted in a “split-S”, keeping positive G. This alarmed me
immediately because, with the turn rate of the F-104, I thought we were
too close to the ground for this to be a safe manoeuvre. I could see that
my “push” had taken me down to 300-400 ft, while Jack had only changed
his aircraft’s attitude but had not lost altitude. As a result the SA-2 scored
a direct hit on his aircraft’s fuselage behind the canopy and electronics
bay, about where the fuel tank was located. A fireball began to expand
outwards and Jack ejected through it. My impression was that he didn’t
have time to eject himself, but was blown out of the aircraft. However, he
separated from the seat and the parachute opened automatically.
