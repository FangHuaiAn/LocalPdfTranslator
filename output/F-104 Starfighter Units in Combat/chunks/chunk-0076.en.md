he October 1958 ceasefire did not mark the end of F-104
operations in Taiwan, for the aircraft eventually became the
RoCAF’s longest-serving fighter. The release of Starfighters from
USAF ADC duties allowed the supply of 22 F-104As, eight F-104Bs and
six F-104Ds to the RoCAF’s 8th TFS in 1960 under Project Alishan 1 – an
eleven-stage programme that imported F-104s from various sources. The
3rd Air Group operated these from Ching Chuan Kang AB, near Taipei.
Many F-104A pilots were trained by the 157th FIS at Congaree AFB,
where there were occasional language-based problems, as Maj Gen Stan
Hood recalled;

When later Starfighter models became available
some F-104As were returned to the USA, many for
re-delivery to Pakistan and Jordan. Their replacements
were 69 F-104Gs and nine TF-104Gs supplied
directly from Lockheed and Canadair production,
delivered between 1963-65.
Renewed hostility in 1967 brought these aircraft
into combat with communist Chinese fighters,
and on 13 January four F-104Gs from a batch
of 27 Canadair-built aircraft tangled with eight
J-6/ MiG-19s. In the ensuing melee air-to-air victories
were claimed by Maj Shih-lin Hu and Capt Bei-puo
Shih, flying F-104Gs 64-17770 (coded 4344) and
64-17773 (coded 4347), respectively. Each claimed a
communist aircraft destroyed, but one F-104G and
its pilot were lost in return.
For Kelly Johnson this clash revealed the strengths
and weaknesses of the F-104 in aerial combat. ‘We
had them on acceleration and we had them on
steady-state altitude, but we could not turn with
them’, he noted. Johnson continued working on a
big-wing, more manoeuvrable version of the F-104
known as the Lancer.
