he Pakistan Air Force (PAF) began F-104 operations on receipt of
the first of 12 F-104As in August 1961 for No 9 Air Superiority
Squadron at Sargodha, Pakistan’s main fighter base. These jets had
been refurbished prior to delivery under US Military Defence Mutual
Assistance Programme (MAP) funding, having been fitted with C-2
upward-firing ejection seats, arresting hooks and more fuel-efficient
J79-GE-11A engines. This uprated powerplant gave these F-104As the best
thrust-to-weight ratio of any Starfighter variant (until the USAF’s 318th
FIS’s F-104As subsequently received the even more powerful J79-GE-19),
the jets having dazzling speed and climb performance. M61 guns, removed
during the aircrafts’ USAF usage, were also re-installed. Removing the gun
had enabled an optional 100-gallon fuel tank to be installed. AIM-9B
missiles could be carried for the air superiority role on ‘Red Dog’ wingtip
launchers. Two F-104Bs (57-1309 and 57-1312) were also supplied.
In 1948, after the partition of India and Pakistan and the initial clash
over Kashmir between the two countries, the Indian Air Force (IAF)
became the first Asian air arm with jet fighters (de Havilland Vampires),
but the PAF’s acquisition of F-104As made it the first with Mach 2
aircraft. Pilots for the elite No 9 Sqn ‘Griffins’ came with considerable
flying time on the F-86F Sabre. Groundcrews, with long experience on
Sabres, achieved 80 per cent in-commission rates during the F-104’s first
five years of service with US assistance.
The first three PAF pilots chosen to fly the F-104 received conversion
training in the USA, and one, Sqn Ldr M Sadruddin (the designated
No 9 Sqn commander) was attached to the 434th TFS at George AFB
and became the first Pakistani to fly at Mach 2. Flt Lts Alauddin ‘Butch’
Ahmed and Mervyn Middlecoat were trained with the 157th FIS, South
Carolina ANG, at McEntire ANG base. This unit was also tasked with
training F-104 pilots from the RoCAF and Japan. Stan Hood had the son
of the famous Adm Isoroku Yamamoto among his students, and rated
him an excellent pilot, although he could never beat Capt Hood at ping-
pong, Japan’s national game.
USAF test pilot Maj Swart Nelson checked out the three US-trained
PAF officers before they flew their F-104As home to Sargodha air station,
where a team of Lockheed ‘tech reps’ provided maintenance advice while
two ANG pilots oversaw conversion training.
