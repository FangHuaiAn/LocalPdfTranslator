LIBYA, IRAQ AND KOSOVO
The Starfighter’s final wartime scenarios both involved F-104s of the
Italian Air Force, which operated the aircraft longer than any other NATO
air arm, and considerably longer than the jet’s 15 years of USAF service.
Its first F-104Gs, selected in preference to the F-4 Phantom II and
Dassault Mirage III, were received in March 1963, and 11 Aeronautica
Militaire Italian (AMI) Stormos eventually flew 136 Fiat-built
F/ RF/ TF-104Gs (plus 12 Lockheed TF-104Gs) and 206 of the Aeritalia-
produced F-104S ‘Spillone’ version. With a versatile air-to-air upload of
AIM-7 or Aspide radar-guided missiles and AIM-9L Sidewinders (but no
gun), and a performance that was still as spectacular as it had been in
1954, the F-104S was limited only by the relatively short range of its
look-down/shoot-down radar and the lack of in-flight refuelling
equipment.
These shortcomings were overlooked when Col Muammar al Gaddafi’s
forces fired several ‘Scud’ missiles towards the Italian island of Lampedusa
following USAF and US Navy air strikes on Libya on 14 April 1986. AMI
Starfighters were immediately moved to bases in southern Italy, their
mission being to protect the US Navy’s LORAN (long range navigation)
installation on Lampedusa and deter any further hostile action by
Gaddafi’s air force or missile batteries. Armed with live weapons, the
F-104s flew CAPs and escorted civilian airliners in areas that were
vulnerable to Libyan intervention.

base, in Turkey, for Operation ACE Guard, which was part of Operation

population of northern Iraq. Their daily missions with the ACE Mobile
Force took the Starfighters over some hostile terrain in challenging winter
weather, but the imagery taken during these flights was very useful when
