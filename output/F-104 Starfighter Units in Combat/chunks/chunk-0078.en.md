When hostilities between Greece and Turkey flared up in 1963 over their
respective areas of occupation of the newly-independent island of Cyprus
the F-104G units were still training. Fighter-bomber duties were therefore
undertaken by Turkey’s F-84G Thunderjet and F-100C/D Super Sabre
squadrons. An uneasy truce was then maintained for ten years, but the
appointment of the Greek Nicos Sampson as president of the country in
July 1974 re-ignited the fighting. Turkey dropped paratroops around the
capital, Nicosia, and mounted a seaborne invasion of the island. Air strikes
were flown by three squadrons of F-100s and the F-104Gs of Filo 141,
hitting targets around Nicosia including the National Guard barracks.
Balikesir’s Filo 191 F-104Gs were moved to Murted to supplement Filo 141
and a second wave of amphibious forces was launched on 21 July, ostensibly
to protect Turkish Cypriot citizens.
As the conflict burgeoned and peace talks stalled, many anticipated
clashes between THK Starfighters and the 170+ examples of the Lockheed
fighter flown by the Greek Air Force – it too had also received batches of
newly-built MAP F-104Gs from 1961 and others passed on by fellow
NATO air forces. However, events for the THK took a different turn.
The Greek Navy sent the landing craft HS Lesvos (L-176) to Paphos,
giving rise to reports that a Greek convoy was being assembled to counter
the Turkish amphibious landing. Grumman S-2E Tracker aircraft were
sent aloft to investigate, and their radar reports seemed to indicate a
substantial enemy naval force, although a daytime reconnaissance flight
by a THK RF-84F Thunderflash pilot found no supporting evidence.
Radar sweeps by a coastal station and further S-2E flights still showed a
potential threat, however, with intercepted Greek radio traffic indicating
that three Hellenic navy destroyers were attempting to join up with
