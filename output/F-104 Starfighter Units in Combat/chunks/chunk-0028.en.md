‘Nothing can set a programme back faster than dumping an aircraft in
the Atlantic somewhere off the Azores on its maiden deployment flight.
This line of reasoning was not entirely objective either – there were eight
pilots who took a rather subjective viewpoint of this aspect of the exercise!
Our greatest problem with the aircraft had been afterburner failures while
in flight. A fix had been made, which an accelerated flight programme on
selected aircraft had verified. Takeoff was on time. The weather was clear,
with a last-quarter moon. This was just as well as the F-104 accelerated
rapidly on takeoff and was extremely hard to spot in the dark, which made
night join-ups difficult.’
Two air-spares returned to Myrtle Beach, leaving six to make three
successful refuellings from Boeing KB-50 tankers that demonstrated how
‘air-to-air hook-ups in the F-104C were comparatively easy’. In Vietnam,
these aircraft would conduct similar refuelling sessions on very many
occasions, and verifying the technique at this early stage was reassuring.
For European deployments the jets’ KB-50 tankers had to be pre-
positioned in set orbits which the F-104 pilots could meet and join. Norm
Filer recalled that with the F-104C, ‘plugging [into the drogue “basket”]
was easier than in the F-100C Super Sabre, but the speed difference
between the tanker and you was much more noticeable. While the F-100C
would just wallow around before dropping off [the tanker], the F-104C
just gave up and fell off suddenly. It did not like to go slow. The only way
to get full tanks if you were carrying all four was to call for a toboggan
descent when things started getting heavy. If you had more than a flight
of four to refuel, and everyone needed a descent to get full tanks, the last
guy on the tanker was almost down in the weeds’.
When jet-powered KC-135A tankers became available they could keep
pace with the fighters and make the crossing with them. Refuelling from
the faster KC-135As was also considerably easier than working with
KB-50s, although a failure of the ‘disconnect’ mechanism once refuelling
was complete could be lethal. The 435th TFS lost one of its F-104Ds
(used for squadron check flights) during a night refuelling session over
Spain. Instead of releasing the nozzle at the end of the F-104D’s stalky
refuelling probe, the tanker’s Flying Boom wrenched it off. The heavy
nozzle promptly flew back into the fighter’s port intake and the engine
caught fire, obliging Maj John Thomas and his back-seat passenger (the
squadron flight surgeon) to eject.
On their own, Starfighters could cruise at around Mach 0.9 with four
external tanks, using an omni-directional VHF radio navigation aid and
their search radars to locate tankers within a 40-mile search range. The
return journey on 2 September was also uneventful, and the deployment
was the first of several to Morón AB by F-104s. For pilots, the journey
(which could last more than 10.5 hours on the non-stop crossings in the
mid-1960s) was made less pleasant by the need to wear bulky, waterproof
rubber ‘poopy suits’ and other survival gear – including a bottle of
stimulant ‘go-pills’ to keep them awake. They also knew that if their single
engine failed the chances of survival in the freezing Atlantic were minimal.

air defence for SAC B-47 Stratojets operating from both Morón and
