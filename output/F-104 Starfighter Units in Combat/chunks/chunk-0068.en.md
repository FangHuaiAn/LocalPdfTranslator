During the third and final week of the war the Sargodha Starfighters
maintained their very effective defensive posture. Rather like the USAF’s
F-104Cs in Vietnam, their presence as a deterrent to enemy interdiction
or interference with air strikes was more important than the actual combat
engagements that they participated in. A typical example occurred on 16
September when four Mystères attacking Pakistani gun emplacements
near Pasrur with T10 rockets curtailed their strike when warned of an
approaching F-104A.
The ‘Griffins’ flew 254 sorties in all, of which 246 saw them flying day
and night air defence CAPs and the rest were escort missions of various
types. Most engagements were fought between the dominant F-86F force
and Indian Hunters, Gnats and Mystères. The fledgling IAF MiG-21
squadron undertook only 80 sorties, its pilots being limited by their lack of
training and experience to flying morale-boosting ‘fighter screen’ sorties that
were meant to deter the Starfighters, which were seen as the greatest threat
to IAF interdictors. No direct contact between MiG-21s and F-104As took
place, although several mutual sightings were reported. Three of the IAF’s
ten MiG-21s were lost to other causes during the 1965 conflict.
Pakistan’s two wartime F-104A losses were not replaced by the USA
due to an arms embargo that was applied to both sides once hostilities
began, although Indian forces were relatively unaffected as they used
little US equipment compared with Pakistan. The combat loss of
