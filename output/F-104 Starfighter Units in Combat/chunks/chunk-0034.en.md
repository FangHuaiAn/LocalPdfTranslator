After President John F Kennedy’s 22 October television address to the
nation, in which he showed photographs of the Soviet missile sites in Cuba,
a US invasion of that country seemed imminent. The 435th TFS pilots had
been led to believe that the crucial time would be 0600 hrs the following
morning, and the alert signal would be telephones ringing continuously in
all the pilots’ rooms at the hotel. As George Wells explained;
‘We all did a little hyperventilating when the phones in all our rooms
rang steadily at 0500 hrs. We literally jumped into our flying suits and ran
out of our rooms, still zipping up the zippers. We were greeted by about a
dozen reporters taking our pictures as rapidly as they could. I guess the
operation was not as big a secret as we had imagined. I rolled out of my
vehicle and almost fell on the ramp as I scrambled for the ladder up to the
cockpit. My crew chief was already there with the auxiliary power unit fired
up. I transmitted on the designated frequency that I was ready to go.
‘I recall this whole event as being sort of surreal. I could not comprehend
the enormity and the implications of what might happen, but I was
mentally prepared to charge ahead into whatever the day had in store for
me. After about 15 minutes we were informed over the radio that the
operation had been cancelled. No matter how willing and even somewhat
eager I was to launch my aeroplane into an unknown possible nuclear
confrontation, I was equally relieved when I was informed that it was not
going to happen, at least at that time.’
President Kennedy’s strategy had forced the Soviet leader, Nikita
Khrushchev, to back down, and put on hold USAF Chief of Staff Gen
Curtis LeMay’s determination to press for an immediate invasion
following the shooting down of a U-2 by a Russian SAM over Cuba.
However, the 435th pilots remained at Key West until 8 December,
completing a 50-day deployment.
