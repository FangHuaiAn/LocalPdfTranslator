Middlecoat, with two underwing Sidewinders and two wingtip tanks,
thundered along the course of the main runway through 40 mm AAA,
firing at parked aircraft that the IAF later claimed were decoys and
egressing over the sea. A No 47 Sqn ‘Black Archers’ MiG-21FL (Type 77),
flown by Flt Lt Bharat Bushan Soni, dived after the Starfighter in
afterburner and fired a K-13 ‘Atoll’ missile from an altitude of 100 ft,
which missed its target.
Unfortunately for Middlecoat the MiG (coded C750) was one of a
handful that had been fitted with the Soviet twin GSh-23 gun installation
in a GP-9 gondola beneath the aircraft. Soni, some 900 ft behind the F-104,
fired three bursts from the short-ranged gun pack and the Starfighter began
to burn. Middlecoat told Habib that he could not reach land and ejected.
His parachute just had time to deploy before his aircraft crashed inverted
into the sea, witnessed by Habib. A rescue boat was sent to search for the
pilot but no trace of Middlecoat was ever found in the shark-infested Gulf
of Kutch. Moments later Habib saw a MiG-21 off his right wing, and he
tried to move in behind it and engage the IAF jet, but a fault developed in
his auto-stabilisation system and he had to break off and escape.
Middlecoat had earlier flown for King Hussein of Jordan during the Six
Day War, and the King asked for the Jordanian flag to be buried with the
pilot if he was ever found, together with the Pakistani flag.
As in 1965, India’s Canberra fleet still posed a formidable threat to
Pakistan’s air bases at night in 1971. Being home to the F-104s, Masroor
was particularly at risk of attack, so a CAP flight of Starfighters was detached
to the nearby Faisal air base in an effort to catch incoming Canberras. It was
during the course of one such CAP flight on 17 December that No 9 Sqn
suffered its last combat loss. Sqn Ldr Bhatti and Flt Lt Samad Changezi,
flying borrowed Jordanian F-104As 56-0839 and 56-0767, respectively, had
been patrolling near the Indian border when they were ordered to orbit
around Mirpur Khas and Naya Chor during a return flight from Faisal to
Masroor. Two Utterlai-based MiG-21FLs had been detected escorting a
flight of four HF-24 Maruts on an army support mission, the six IAF jets
heading straight for the F-104s at an altitude of 10,000 ft.
MiG-21 pilots often flew seek-and-destroy missions at high altitude, with
two Hunters or Maruts as bait to lure PAF interceptors into action. However,
they usually reported little reaction from the Pakistani bases. This time, both
pairs met in a head-on ‘blow through’ and then turned in behind each other.
Changezi, who saw the MiGs first, broke away from Bhatti and turned in
behind the lead MiG. He then closed to within gun range.
The need to use wingtip tanks for increased endurance had ruled out
Sidewinder carriage on the RJAF F-104As, which had not been modified
to carry missiles on their underwing pylons. External fuel tanks were not
usually carried in combat due to the extra drag they created.
The second MiG-21FL (flown by Flt Lt Aruna Datta) moved in
behind Changezi and fired an ‘Atoll’, but it was launched outside correct
parameters. Moments after Changezi had ignored Bhatti’s order to
jettison his tanks and disengage, a second ‘Atoll’ smashed into 56-0767
and the jet exploded, killing the young pilot. The MiGs headed back to
the border and Bhatti, low on fuel, had to return to Masroor. Two more
