This complex system caused inevitable delays in setting up interceptions.
Canberra pilots could evade the CAPs by flying below 1000 ft, where they
were lost in ground clutter to the F-104As’ radars. PAF pilots had to fly
even lower, hoping to acquire the Canberras visually against a sky
background, but knowing that their radars could set off the tail warning
radar, code-named Orange Putter, fitted to Canberras to alert their crews
to imminent interceptions.
The F-104 was greatly respected by IAF crews, who had nicknamed it
‘badmash’ (‘hooligan’) before the war. On 3 September 1965 eight No 23
Sqn Gnats were detached to Pathankot to escort four Mystères that were
supposed to be medium altitude ‘bait’ for PAF Sabre pilots. The Gnats, led
by RAF-trained Sqn Ldrs William ‘Johnny’ Greene and Trevor Keelor,
trailed the Mystères at an altitude of 100 ft, ready to spring an ambush. A
CAP of two F-86Fs was sent to intercept them. Keelor damaged Sqn Ldr
Yousuf Khan’s F-86F (claimed as the first IAF aerial victory of the war, but
denied in PAF records), and Flt Lt V S Pathania was about to fire on a second
when two Starfighters, scrambled to rescue the F-86Fs, dived into the Gnat
formation at 500 knots and had to overshoot, scattering the IAF fighters
but presenting a fleeting target for Flt Lt Krishnaswamy’s 30 mm guns.

the Starfighter, and the IAF pilots then headed for base as their fuel was
