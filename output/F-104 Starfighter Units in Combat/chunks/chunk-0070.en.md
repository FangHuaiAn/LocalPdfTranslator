Following the 1965 conflict with India over Kashmir, the F-104As’
underwing fuel tank pylons were modified to carry LAU-3B AIM-9B
launchers so that the wingtip stations could be used for fuel tanks, which
caused less drag than the underwing type. Air Vice Marshal Farooq Umar
recalled;
‘I joined the F-104 squadron in 1962 as part of the second batch of
PAF pilots. Following the 1965 war the underwing pylons were adapted
to carry Sidewinders for operational benefits. This was done primarily by
Sqn Ldr Hakim Ullah, a combat pilot who later led the F-104 unit.
Although not an engineer, Ullah, who was a remarkable innovator, did all
the planning and drawings for this modification, and it performed well.’
Umar was also impressed by the F-104A’s radar, which in his opinion
was perfectly adequate for the jet’s air superiority role;
‘It had a range of 25 miles [the same as the terminal radars protecting
PAF bases], but being a helical scan type it would pick up a lot of clutter
at lower altitudes. In spite of the best tuning it was difficult to identify
targets from the ground clutter, however.’
Attempts were also made by both local technicians and a PAF F-104
pilot to improve the half-mile operational range of the Starfighter’s inbuilt
infrared detection set. Using liquid nitrogen to cool its indium antimonide
infrared cell, the set’s detection and lock-on range was apparently increased
to ten kilometres, but icing problems forced the abandonment of this
project. Another initiative involved the testing of a short-range, low-
altitude radar detection (SLARD) set to improve target acquisition nearer
the ground. It used two nose-mounted sensors to give detection capability
at a range of up to ten miles and 30 degrees on either side of the jet. The
equipment occupied too much cockpit space, however, and further
development was curtailed.
THE THIRTEEN-DAY WAR
Lacking intervention from the international community, India (which had
signed a defence pact with the Soviet Union in August 1971) decided in
December 1971 to intervene in the conflict over Bangladesh and began a
massive build-up of forces along the East Pakistan border. Air action had
actually begun on 16 October when IAF Canberras attacked targets in West
Pakistan. Around 40 Canberras remained in Indian service, and they had
been joined by around 175 MiG-21FL ‘Trishuls’ (Tridents) equipping nine
squadrons. There were also 150 Sukhoi Su-7BMs, 160 Gnats, 100 Hunters,
domestically produced HF-24 Maruts and 40 Mystères, allowing the IAF
to field around 730 combat aircraft. Of these, the Hunters and Su-7BMs
would sustain the majority of the casualties during the renewed conflict.
Pakistan, deprived of Western re-armament by embargoes, had acquired
70 Sidewinder-armed Chinese Shenyang F-6s (MiG-19s) and 90
ex-Luftwaffe Canadair Sabre 6s via Iran. The Chinese deal was allegedly
connected to the loan (and eventual return) of a disassembled PAF F-104A
to China for technical examination. France also supplied 18 Mirage IIIEPs
to replace F-104As, and in September 1970 the USA offered a ‘one-time
