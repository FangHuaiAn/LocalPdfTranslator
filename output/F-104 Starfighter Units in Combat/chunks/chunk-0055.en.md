The only real chance the pilot had of avoiding being hit by an SA-2 was
to see it in time to perform the hazardous last-second dive and turn needed
to out-manoeuvre the missile. In any case, the RHAW was itself susceptible
to many irrelevant transmissions that merely created distracting false alarms.
ECM jamming pods like the QRC-160-1 carried by F-105s and F-4s were
a much more effective method of defeating SAMs electronically, and one
F-104C was test-flown at Korat RTAFB with a QRC-160-1 on the
centreline pylon. The pod was designed for use on all four aircraft in a strike
flight, where the jamming power could be multiplied sufficiently in a
carefully measured ‘pod formation’ to provide real protection. It was far less
effective on a single aircraft and, in any case, the pods were scarce, with
F-105 units taking precedence in the supply chain. No extra pods were
funded to protect F-104Cs, and the AN/APR-25/26 units were removed
when the aircraft returned to the USA.
Escort missions over the North continued into the summer of 1966.
On 22 August a 388th TFW F-105 flight approached its target near
Hanoi along ‘Thud Ridge’, dropped its M117 bombs and was met by two
MiG-17s as it egressed. Two F-105Ds were damaged by gunfire, and they
called in the F-104C MiGCAP flight. Apparently, the Starfighters did not
receive the call and their CAP orbit placed them too far north of the target
area to see the MiGs, whose controller had clearly vectored his interceptors
past the MiGCAP for a clear shot at the F-105s. 67
