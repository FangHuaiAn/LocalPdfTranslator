CUBA
ADC’s ‘re-possession’ of its F-104As was a recognition of the aircraft’s
usefulness in meeting the threats associated with the Cuban Missile Crisis.
The sighting of two SA-2 SAM sites in that country on 29 August 1962
began a stand off between President John F Kennedy and Soviet Premier
Nikita Khrushchev that required numerous TAC units to deploy to the
southeastern USA. The 479th TFW also recalled two of its Europe-
deployed units early in 1962, leaving one at Morón, and relocated them
to Naval Air Station (NAS) Key West (previously known as Boca Chica),
Florida, to counter possible air attacks by the 125 Soviet-supplied MiGs
believed to be based on Cuba.
The prospect of meeting Cuban MiGs in the ‘hot-rodded’, lightweight
F-104 gave rise to some intensive air-to-air practice with Tyndall AFB-
based F-106A Delta Darts. Realising that subsonic turning fights were to
be avoided, the F-104 pilots relied on sustained high speed and use of the
vertical dimension wherever possible. Tactics evolved whereby a flight of
F-104s could vary the flight lead position, depending on which pilot had
the best chance of a shot, rather than adhering to the standard USAF
‘fighting wing’ with one ‘shooter’ leading the four-aircraft flight and
taking the firing opportunities. This approach required a training
programme that qualified all new pilots as flight leaders. In Vietnam, this
