eorge Wells took his first ‘dollar ride’ as a cadet in a Lockheed T-33
at Bergstrom AFB, Texas, in 1955, and it was ‘the single most
impressive event that I had ever experienced up to that point in
my life’. Six years later, after a tour on F-100s with the 48th TFW, George
reported to Victorville, California, to join the 435th TFS/479th TFW flying
F-104Cs. His first reactions to the aircraft were extremely positive;
‘There were very few fighter pilots in the air force who would not have
given an arm and a leg to be assigned to an F-104 squadron. I would hear
many stories of how political influence and other extraordinary efforts
were used to get the assignment. As I taxied out on my first solo flight, I
did not experience the usual feeling of apprehension. Instead, it seemed
that this airplane was built specifically for me. I felt totally comfortable
and I had not even arrived at the takeoff end of the runway yet. I found
that its flying characteristics and performance were far superior to the
F-100. The F-104 was really fun to fly, and it was just about the fastest
thing in the air back in the early 1960s.’
F-104C production reinstated the M61 cannon, and its use became
part of the syllabus at George AFB. Wells was impressed by the weapon’s
accuracy while he qualified in air-to-air gunnery;
‘This was accomplished by shooting at a silver honeycombed dart that was
towed 1500 ft behind another F-104. My instructor, Ray Krasovitch, who
was one of the best F-104 pilots, in my estimation, in the world, explained
the techniques to shoot holes in the dart and how the radar worked. The
radar in my aeroplane did work, which was pretty rare I was told.
‘Ray instructed me that when a little blip on the sight reticle appeared
it was telling me that I was 3000 ft from the dart. Since the gun was
certified to be accurate at 3000 ft I could start shooting any time I had
my sight lined up properly on the target. The thought crossed my mind
that I should wait until I was a little closer to the target, but since every
parameter had been met I elected to hose off a few rounds just to get a
little practice in pulling the trigger on the control stick. To my great
surprise I not only scored hits on the dart but completely destroyed it.
The honeycomb structure was covered with an aluminium coating, and
the dart formed a sparkling, glittering cloud in the bright sunshine as it
descended to the desert below.’
His early experience of firing live GAR-8 (AIM-9B) missiles was less
fortunate. He and Ray Krasovitch were told to down a T-33 target drone
for a TAC film called Full Scope, which was intended to show the
command’s capability and release much-needed funds at a time when the
majority of USAF dollars were going to SAC. The pilots were required to
fire at the target, which was flying steadily at around 1000 ft to make it
more visible to the ground-based movie cameras. Distracted by ground-
heat, the old-stock missiles missed the target every time during two days
of filming until one of Ray’s grazed the T-33’s rudder without exploding
and the target drone crashed – out of the cameras’ view. 39
