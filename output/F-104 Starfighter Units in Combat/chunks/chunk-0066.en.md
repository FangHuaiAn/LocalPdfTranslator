The ‘Griffins’’ contact with IAF aircraft was sporadic during the
following week. Flt Lt Hakimullah, patrolling over Indian terrain on 11
September but low on fuel, was warned that two sections of IAF fighters,
including two Gnats, had sighted him. He dived towards them and lined
up for a Sidewinder shot, but he was outside AIM-9B launch parameters.
Re-positioning his aircraft behind one of the Gnats, he received a positive
launch tone for the missile, but he also heard a further warning of two
more IAF interceptors.
Hakimullah identified the new threats as MiG-21s and turned towards
them, but he noticed that his fuel was worryingly low and so briefly
engaged afterburner to evade them. Another pair of MiG-21F-13s was
then seen approaching him head-on, and he used the F-104A’s formidable
acceleration to dive beneath them at supersonic speed, out of Indian
airspace. Indian records suggest that he may have mistaken Hunters for
MiG-21s, and it would certainly have been unusual for four of the modest
IAF MiG-21 force to have been patrolling one small area simultaneously.
Hunters were very active that day and two other F-104 pilots passed close
to No 7 Sqn jets attacking the Lahore-Kasur road bridge in their CAP
area. Hakimullah landed at a forward airfield at Risalawala and his fuel-
starved engine flamed out whilst the jet was rolling down the runway.

Canberras at night. Although the twin-engined bombers were large and
limited to speeds of around 380 knots, they could operate below Pakistan’s
radar coverage and enter its airspace along the 1000-mile border separating
